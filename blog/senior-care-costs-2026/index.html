Assisted Living in 2026: What $5,419/Month Actually Covers

The median figure masks significant variation in what is included. Assisted living pricing typically falls into two structures:

All-inclusive pricing

One monthly rate covers room, board, personal care assistance, meals, activities, and basic utilities. More common in continuing care retirement communities (CCRCs) and higher-end facilities.

A la carte / tiered pricing

Base room rate plus care level add-ons. Personal care services (bathing, medication management, mobility assistance) are billed separately based on assessed need. This is the more common structure for freestanding assisted living.

What the $5,419 median includes varies by community. The questions that matter:

  • Is medication management included or billed per medication?
  • What care level tier is the base rate?
  • Is incontinence care bundled or separate?
  • What happens to pricing when care needs increase?

These are not fine-print questions. They are the difference between a $5,400/month budget and an $8,200/month reality.

Memory Care in 2026: The $6,690 Baseline — and What Drives It Higher

Memory care pricing at $6,690/month reflects the higher staff-to-resident ratios, secured environment requirements, and specialized programming that dementia care demands. The premium over standard assisted living (currently about $1,271/month nationally) is real and justified.

But that premium varies considerably by:

Staff credentialing

Communities with certified dementia practitioners on staff typically charge more. The question is whether that investment is visible and verifiable.

Programming depth

Structured cognitive engagement programs (music therapy, reminiscence therapy, sensory stimulation) affect both pricing and outcomes. Not all memory care units offer the same depth.

Community size

Smaller, purpose-built memory care communities typically cost more than memory care wings within larger assisted living facilities — but often deliver measurably better outcomes for moderate-to-advanced dementia.

Staff turnover rates

This is the number aggregators never publish. High turnover in memory care is both a quality signal and a cost driver (residents require re-acclimation every time). Carepriced surfaces staff stability data where communities disclose it.

Home Care at $34/Hour: The Math Families Get Wrong

Home care pricing is deceptively simple on the surface: $34/hour, national median. The math gets complicated fast.

ScenarioWeekly HoursAnnual Cost
Supplemental support10 hrs/week~$17,700/year
Part-time companion care20 hrs/week~$35,400/year
Full-time non-medical care40 hrs/week~$70,700/year
Around-the-clock supervision168 hrs/week~$297,000/year

The $34 median is for non-medical home care (companion care, personal care assistance). Home health care — which involves licensed nurses, physical therapists, and skilled medical services — commands a separate rate, typically billed through Medicare or Medicaid for qualifying needs.

The critical distinction most families miss: Home care agencies vs. independent caregivers. Agency rates run higher ($30–$45/hour in most markets) but include worker compensation coverage, background screening, caregiver backup when a regular aide is unavailable, and supervision. Independent caregivers run $15–$25/hour but shift legal employer responsibilities to the family.

Neither is universally better. The right choice depends on care needs, budget, and the family capacity to manage caregiver employment.

What Aggregators Cannot Tell You — And Carepriced Can

The major senior care aggregator sites (A Place for Mom, Caring.com, Seniorly, and their competitors) serve a real function: they have catalogued a massive number of facilities and made them searchable. That is genuinely useful.

But their business model creates structural blind spots:

Referral fees distort recommendations

Aggregators earn referral fees from communities when a family they referred moves in. This is disclosed, but it shapes which communities appear prominently and which do not. Communities that do not pay referral fees may not appear at all.

Pricing data is not verified

Listed prices on aggregator platforms are self-reported by communities and frequently outdated. A Place for Mom’s own report notes the difficulty of standardizing pricing data across thousands of communities.

Reviews lack verification

Who left that five-star review? Aggregator review systems generally cannot verify that reviewers are actual residents or family members — and the communities pay the platform, not the reviewers.

Staff credentials are invisible

You can see star ratings. You can read testimonials. You generally cannot see staff certification status, turnover rates, inspection histories, or deficiency citations — the data that actually predicts care quality.

The Local Cost Research You Should Actually Do

Before any facility tour, before any conversation with a placement counselor, know your local market:

  1. Get the actual rate sheet — Not the starting-from price. The full rate sheet including care level add-ons. Any community that refuses to share this before a tour is telling you something.
  2. Ask about rate increase history — How much have rates increased in each of the last three years? This is public information for nonprofit communities; for-profits are less likely to share it but should be asked.
  3. Understand the move-in vs. move-up cost — Entry-level pricing and mid-care-level pricing are often very different. Know what you will pay when care needs increase.
  4. Compare care levels across communities — A Level 1 care package at Community A may include services that Community B bills at Level 3. You are not comparing apples to apples on base rates alone.
  5. Check inspection records — State licensing agencies publish inspection reports and deficiency citations. This data exists. Use it.
