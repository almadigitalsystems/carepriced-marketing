The 50-State Assisted Living Price Map: Where 2026 Is Most Expensive and Most Affordable

Published May 8, 2026 — Carepriced.com

Reading time: 9 minutes

TL;DR

  • The national median for assisted living in 2026 ranges from $5,419 to $6,386 per month, depending on the source.
  • The cheapest state (Louisiana at $3,983/mo) costs less than half of the most expensive (Washington DC at $8,960/mo).
  • Memory care adds another 20–30% on top of standard assisted living rates.
  • Occupancy hit 89.1% in Q4 2025 — an 18-quarter growth streak — and operators are passing 3–8% annual rate hikes through to families.
  • The state you choose can change the cost of care by $200,000–$400,000 over a 4-year stay.

The Question Families Almost Never Get a Straight Answer To

It's the week before Mother's Day. A daughter in Phoenix calls her brother in Charlotte, and the call goes the way these calls always go.

"Mom can't keep living alone. We have to do something."

The "something" almost always means assisted living. And the very first question — what does it actually cost? — is the one nobody seems willing to answer in plain numbers.

Industry websites bury the figure behind a phone form. Tour guides quote a number that doesn't include memory care, medication management, or the second-person fee. Brochures show "starting at" prices that almost nobody actually pays.

So we built this guide. Every figure on this page comes from a published 2026 industry source. Every state is on the map. Every assumption is on the table.

If you are evaluating assisted living for a parent right now — for the 10,000 Americans turning 65 every single day (Pew Research Center) — this is your starting point.

The National Picture: $5,419 to $6,386 a Month, and Climbing

There is no single "national price" for assisted living. The three most-cited 2026 industry surveys disagree by almost a thousand dollars a month:

Source2026 National Median (Monthly)
A Place for Mom (APFM) 2026 Cost Report$5,419
SeniorLiving.org 2026 Cost Guide$6,313
TheSeniorList 2026$6,386

Why the gap? The methodologies differ. APFM samples from its own family-referral network — heavily weighted toward smaller, rural, and value-tier communities. SeniorLiving.org and TheSeniorList survey across the broader market, including premium urban properties.

For most families budgeting realistically, the honest answer is: plan for $6,000–$6,500 a month for a standard one-bedroom assisted living unit in 2026, and assume year-over-year increases of 3–8%.

That's before memory care. We'll come back to that.

Why Prices Keep Rising

Three forces are squeezing every assisted living operator in the country:

  1. Demand is unprecedented. The U.S. is home to roughly 73 million baby boomers (U.S. Census Bureau, 2020 estimate). By 2030, all baby boomers will be 65 or older, and 1 in 5 Americans will be retirement age (U.S. Census Bureau, Demographic Turning Points for the United States). The oldest boomers turn 80 in 2026, opening the assisted-living window for the largest cohort in U.S. history.
  2. Occupancy is at a record high. The National Investment Center for Seniors Housing & Care (NIC) reported 89.1% occupancy in Q4 2025 — the 18th consecutive quarter of growth. When buildings are full, prices rise.
  3. Operators can't hire. 94% of senior living operators report active recruiting difficulty (NIC MAP, Q4 2025). Caregiver wages are the single largest operating cost, and they have been rising faster than general inflation in nearly every state.

Translation: the supply curve is tight, the demand curve is steep, and the labor curve is brutal. Prices are not coming down.

The 50-State Map

The full map is at the top of this article — color scale runs from the lightest umber tone (Louisiana, $3,983) to the darkest (Washington DC, $8,960) on the Carepriced 2026 sequential ramp. Source: A Place for Mom, 2026 Pricing Guide.

The map tells the story at a glance. There are essentially three Americas of assisted living pricing:

  • Premium America (>$6,000/mo): Washington DC, the Northeast Corridor, Alaska, the Pacific Northwest's coastal metros, and pockets of the Mid-Atlantic.
  • Middle America ($5,000–$6,000/mo): the upper Midwest, most of the Mountain West, the South Atlantic, and California.
  • Affordable America (<$5,000/mo): the Deep South, parts of the Plains, the Southwest, and pockets of Appalachia.

Where you place your parent matters. A move from Louisiana to Washington DC changes the cost by nearly $60,000 a year ($59,724 to be exact) — and that's before memory care premiums. The care quality, on a per-dollar basis, is often comparable.

The top of the list reflects two structural patterns. Washington DC tops the table on a combination of dense urban real estate, premium-tier labor markets, and a federal-employee tenant base that supports premium pricing. The next six are clustered tightly across the Northeast (Vermont, New Jersey, Massachusetts, Maine, Delaware, Connecticut), where real estate, regulation, and labor all run hot. Alaska is the only non-Northeast state in the top eight — its caregiver wages are pushed up by a small labor pool, long winters, and competition from oil-and-gas employers. Oregon rounds out the top 10 on the strength of its Portland metro and a tightening Pacific Northwest senior housing market.

The cheapest tier shares a common profile: lower real estate costs, lower caregiver wage floors, lighter state regulatory regimes, and more abundant land. None of these factors automatically translate to lower care quality. The state regulators in Louisiana, Mississippi, and the Dakotas run inspection programs that are, in several published comparisons, more transparent than those in Massachusetts or New York.

What Drives the Spread

Why does the same standard of care cost more than twice as much in Washington DC as in Louisiana? Four forces explain almost all of it.

1. Caregiver Wage Floors

Assisted living is, fundamentally, a labor business. Roughly 60–70% of operating cost is staffing. State minimum wages, union pressure, and competing employers (hospitals, retail, hospitality) all push the wage floor up. Caregiver wage floors range from roughly $13/hour in low-cost states like Mississippi to $23/hour in Massachusetts (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics, May 2024; industry surveys). DC's wage floor sits in the same Northeast-premium band as Massachusetts. That difference is most of the gap.

2. Real Estate

Assisted living is also a real estate business. Land, construction, and property tax all flow through to monthly rent. Premium-tier markets (DC, NJ, MA, the Northeast Corridor more broadly) cost two to four times more per square foot to build and operate.

3. Regulation

State-level regulations for staffing ratios, training hours, building codes, and inspection frequency vary dramatically. New York and California require staffing ratios that effectively double the labor cost of a comparably sized facility in Texas or Tennessee.

4. Occupancy

When buildings are full, operators raise rates. Period. The 89.1% national occupancy figure (NIC, Q4 2025) is the highest in 18 quarters. In premium-tier markets, occupancy is running above 92%, and rate increases of 6–8% in 2026 are common. In affordable-tier markets, occupancy is closer to 86–88%, and operators are holding the line at 3–4%.

The Hidden Multiplier: Memory Care

Here is the cost most families don't see coming.

Standard assisted living assumes a resident who can manage basic activities of daily living with light support — medication reminders, bathing assistance, meal preparation. Once cognitive decline progresses to the point where a resident needs a secured memory care wing, the price jumps.

National 2026 medians for memory care:

  • Memory care monthly median: $5,430–$6,450 (TheSeniorList 2026; SeniorLiving.org 2026)
  • Premium over standard assisted living: 20–30%

In Washington DC, that means memory care can run $11,000+ per month. Across the Northeast premium cluster (Vermont, New Jersey, Massachusetts), $9,000–$9,500 per month is typical. In Louisiana or Alabama, around $5,000–$5,500 per month.

The trap: families budget for "assisted living" at age 80, then are blindsided when the facility recommends a transition to memory care at age 83. The same family that was comfortable at $5,400/mo is suddenly looking at $7,000/mo for the next phase of care — and that phase often lasts 4–7 years.

Build the memory care premium into your budget from day one. It is the single most common financial surprise in long-term care planning.

What This Means for Your Family: A Decision Framework

If you are evaluating assisted living right now, run the decision through these four questions in order.

1. What is the realistic monthly cost in the state where you'll actually place your parent?

Not the national median. Not the "starting at" number on the brochure. The 2026 state median for the metro you are looking at, plus a 5% buffer for next year's rate increase. Use Carepriced's by-state calculator to get the figure for your state in 30 seconds.

2. What is the memory care multiplier you should plan for?

Assume your parent will eventually need memory care, even if they don't today. Add 25% to your assisted living budget as the working number for years 3–7.

3. What is the all-in cost over a realistic time horizon?

The average length of stay in assisted living is approximately 22 months (National Center for Assisted Living), with total long-term care needs often spanning multiple years across in-home support, assisted living, and memory care. A reasonable planning baseline is to multiply your monthly figure by 48 months to capture a likely AL → memory care transition.

4. Where does the math actually work?

Run the math in three states: where your parent lives now, where the closest adult child lives, and the lowest-cost state in your region. The gap is often $200,000–$400,000 over a 4-year stay. That is real money, and it is worth a difficult family conversation about geography.
