Memory care cost in Charlotte, NC (2025): the honest median is $5,900/month

If you're pricing memory care in Charlotte this year, the honest median is $5,900 per month — about $1,150 above standard assisted living in the same metro. Not a range. Not "starting at." A real-world median, before behavioral-care add-ons. North Carolina's MC pricing tracks slightly below the national median because the state's senior-care market runs more value-oriented than Atlanta or Tampa. Below: three published sources, what changes by sub-area, and how North Carolina's CAP/DA and State-County SA programs change the math.

Charlotte sub-area pricing — what changes by neighborhood

Within the Charlotte metro, base monthly memory care varies by roughly $2,000/month from the lowest sub-area to the highest.

SouthPark and Ballantyne

Premium MC; lower caregiver ratios; high amenity load. Base monthly typically $6,800–$7,300. Several luxury-tier secured units clear $7,300 with advanced behavioral care.

Dilworth, Myers Park, and Eastover

Mid-premium MC. Base monthly typically $6,200–$6,700. Newer hospital-system-affiliated MC units (Atrium Health, Novant) drive this band.

Uptown and South End

Mid-tier MC. Base monthly typically $5,900–$6,300. Newer urban-format secured units, growing inventory.

University City and northeast Charlotte

Slightly below metro median. Base monthly typically $5,500–$5,900. Mid-tier MC; newer mid-priced communities.

Concord, Huntersville, and Cornelius (north metro)

Mid-tier and value-tier MC mix. Base monthly typically $5,300–$5,700. Newer master-planned community MC; mid-tier.

Gastonia, Monroe, and outer suburbs

Lowest sub-area median. Base monthly typically $5,100–$5,400. Smaller secured units, longer drive from Charlotte's medical centers.

What pushes your Charlotte memory care bill above the median

Memory care add-ons differ from standard AL because the secured baseline already includes a lot of what AL bills as "extra." But the bill still moves:

  • Behavioral care upgrade: $400–$1,000/month. Most relevant when a resident has specific behaviors (wandering, sundowning, exit-seeking) that require additional one-on-one support.
  • Medication management: $200–$400/month if billed separately. Often bundled into MC base in Charlotte — confirm in writing.
  • Incontinence supplies and care: $100–$300/month.
  • Hospice overlay (when needed): Typically $0 additional from the facility (hospice is Medicare-billed).
  • Community fee (one-time): $1,500–$5,000 at move-in. Rarely refundable.
  • Annual rent increase: 3–6% per year on the base.

Realistic median + likely behavioral add-ons for a moderate-care Charlotte MC resident: $6,400–$7,000/month all-in. Heavy-care residents in SouthPark or Ballantyne often clear $7,300.

North Carolina CAP/DA and State-County SA: who qualifies and how it works

North Carolina's Medicaid coverage for memory care works through two programs that families often use in combination: CAP/DA (Community Alternatives Program for Disabled Adults) for in-home and community-based services, and State-County Special Assistance (SA) for Adult Care Home placement payments. We're not a Medicaid-planning service. But not flagging the timing question would be dishonest.

Why these programs matter more in memory care than in AL:

  • Memory care stays are typically longer and monthlies are higher, so personal assets are drawn down faster than in standard AL.
  • CAP/DA has a documented waitlist; planning done early opens more options.
  • Not every Charlotte MC community accepts State-County SA, and among those that do, the SA payment rate is below market — meaning families often co-pay the gap, especially for premium SouthPark or Ballantyne placements.

Important North Carolina nuance: CAP/DA is community-based — it does not directly pay an MC community the way Florida SMMC LTC or Texas STAR+PLUS do. State-County SA is the separate program that supplements payment to Adult Care Homes (the NC licensure category that includes most MC communities). The SA monthly rate is well below the Charlotte MC median, so the gap-payment math is the family's responsibility.

Financial eligibility (2025): CAP/DA uses 300% of SSI federal benefit rate (roughly $2,901/month income limit) plus the $2,000 single-applicant asset limit; SA has its own income standard tied to the SA payment rate. The home is generally exempt with intent-to-return; North Carolina operates Medicaid Estate Recovery.

Medical eligibility: the applicant must require a "nursing facility level of care" as assessed by NC DHHS.

If memory care is a likely 2–4 year commitment for your family, get the NC DHHS eligibility screening initiated in the first 90 days of the diagnosis. Talk to an elder law attorney about North Carolina's look-back rules before you spend down.

Six questions to ask when you tour a Charlotte memory care community

  1. What's your daytime caregiver-to-resident ratio in the memory care unit? This is the cost driver. Anything over 1:8 deserves follow-up questions.
  2. What's your night ratio in the memory care wing? Day ratios matter, but night ratios are where most quiet incidents happen — sundowning, wandering, falls.
  3. How is the behavioral-care fee assessed and how often is it reassessed? Ask for the points sheet.
  4. What's included in MC base versus what's an add-on? Walk through the worksheet above line-by-line.
  5. Do you accept State-County Special Assistance (SA), and what's the cap on SA-funded MC beds? Some Charlotte MC communities cap SA beds because the SA payment rate is below market.
  6. Is your community licensed as an Adult Care Home with a memory care endorsement, and what does that mean for your staffing requirements? NC's licensure category affects staffing minimums for MC; confirm the specific requirements.

If a community won't itemize the bill, won't disclose the night caregiver ratio, or won't answer the State-County SA question clearly, that's a signal worth weighing.
