Assisted living cost in Charlotte, NC (2025): the honest median is $4,750/month

If you're pricing assisted living in Charlotte this year, the honest median is $4,750 per month. Not a range. Not "starting at." A real-world median, before care-level fees and add-ons. Charlotte tracks slightly below the national median ($5,000) because North Carolina's senior-care market is more value-oriented than Atlanta or Tampa. Below: three published sources, what changes by sub-area, and the line-items most families don't see until move-in.

Charlotte sub-area pricing — what changes by neighborhood

The Charlotte metro spans Mecklenburg, Cabarrus, and Union counties. Within the metro, base monthly varies by roughly $1,700/month from the lowest sub-area to the highest.

SouthPark and Ballantyne

Premium properties, larger units, amenity-driven communities. Base monthly typically $5,500–$6,100. Several luxury-tier communities clear $6,100 with care fees layered in.

Dilworth, Myers Park, and Eastover

Mid-premium. Base monthly typically $5,100–$5,600. Older established neighborhoods with newer hospital-system-affiliated communities (Atrium Health, Novant).

Uptown and South End

Mid-tier. Base monthly typically $4,700–$5,100. Newer urban-format inventory; less common building type for AL but growing.

University City and northeast Charlotte

Slightly below metro median. Base monthly typically $4,400–$4,800. Mid-tier inventory and newer mid-priced communities; often the best value for moderate care needs.

Concord, Huntersville, and Cornelius (north metro)

Mid-tier and value-tier mix. Base monthly typically $4,300–$4,700. Newer master-planned community AL; mid-tier.

Gastonia, Monroe, and outer suburbs

Lowest sub-area median. Base monthly typically $3,900–$4,300. Smaller communities, fewer amenities, longer drive from Charlotte's medical centers.

What pushes your Charlotte assisted living bill above the median

The $4,750 base is just the rent + meals + basic services line. The real bill is the base plus add-ons:

  • Care-level fee: $400–$1,200/month, assessed at move-in based on a points system. Most Charlotte communities reassess every 90 days.
  • Medication management: $200–$400/month if staff administers daily meds. Rarely included in base.
  • Incontinence supplies and care: $100–$300/month.
  • Two-person transfer: $300–$600/month if your family member can't transfer independently.
  • Community fee (one-time): $1,500–$5,000 at move-in. Rarely refundable.
  • Annual rent increase: 3–6% per year on the base — North Carolina runs slightly under the national average rate of increases.

Realistic median + likely add-ons for a moderate-care Charlotte resident: $5,300–$5,900/month all-in. Heavy-care residents in SouthPark or Ballantyne often clear $6,100.

North Carolina CAP/DA Medicaid: who qualifies and how it works

If private-pay isn't sustainable, North Carolina's Medicaid long-term care program for community-based care is CAP/DA (Community Alternatives Program for Disabled Adults). CAP/DA is North Carolina's primary 1915(c) Medicaid waiver for adults who meet a nursing facility level of care but want to receive services at home or in a community setting. We're not a Medicaid-planning service. But not mentioning CAP/DA would be dishonest — for many Charlotte families, it's the difference between staying in community and moving to a nursing facility.

Important North Carolina nuance: CAP/DA is community-based and home-focused — it's not a direct "pay the AL community" program the way Florida's SMMC LTC or Texas's STAR+PLUS work. North Carolina's State-County Special Assistance (SA) program is the one that supplements payment for Adult Care Homes (the NC term for many AL communities). Many Charlotte families use a combination: CAP/DA for in-home services, and SA if/when AL becomes necessary.

Two things many North Carolina families don't know early enough:

  • CAP/DA has a documented waitlist. Even financially-eligible applicants commonly wait several months for a slot.
  • Not every Charlotte-area AL accepts SA payment — and the SA monthly payment rate is meaningfully below the metro median, so even at SA-accepting facilities, families often co-pay the gap.

Financial eligibility (2025):

  • Countable assets: at or below $2,000 for a single applicant
  • Monthly income: limits vary by program; CAP/DA uses 300% of SSI federal benefit rate (roughly $2,901/month) and SA has its own income standard
  • The home is generally exempt with intent-to-return; North Carolina operates Medicaid Estate Recovery

Medical eligibility: the applicant must require a "nursing facility level of care" as assessed by NC DHHS.

If AL is going to be a 2+ year commitment for your family, get the NC DHHS eligibility screening initiated in the first 90 days of the search, regardless of current financial position. Talk to an elder law attorney about the look-back rules before you spend down.

Six questions to ask when you tour a Charlotte assisted living community

  1. What's the last 3 years of base rent increases? If they hesitate, that's a signal worth weighing.
  2. How is the care-level fee assessed and how often is it reassessed? Ask for the points sheet. Ask what triggers a mid-year increase.
  3. What's included in base versus what's an add-on? Walk through the worksheet above line-by-line.
  4. Do you accept State-County Special Assistance (SA), and what's the cap on SA-funded beds? SA is the NC program that helps pay for Adult Care Home placement, but the per-resident rate is below market — confirm whether the facility caps SA-funded beds.
  5. Is your community licensed as an Adult Care Home or Family Care Home, and what does that mean for your staffing requirements? NC has different licensure tiers; the answer affects staffing ratios and care-level capabilities.
  6. What's your 30-day notice and refund policy if we move out or pass away? Some communities pro-rate; some don't.

If a community won't itemize the bill, won't show you the rate increase history, or won't answer the State-County SA question clearly, that's a signal worth weighing.
