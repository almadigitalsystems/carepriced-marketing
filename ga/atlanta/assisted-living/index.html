Assisted living cost in Atlanta, GA (2025): the honest median is $5,000/month

If you're pricing assisted living in Atlanta this year, the honest median is $5,000 per month. Not a range. Not "starting at." A real-world median, before care-level fees and add-ons. Atlanta's metro spans Buckhead luxury, Decatur mid-tier, and far-suburb value tiers, so the sub-area swing is significant. Below: three published sources, what changes by neighborhood, and the line-items most families don't see until move-in.

Atlanta sub-area pricing — what changes by neighborhood

Metro Atlanta spans Fulton, DeKalb, Cobb, Gwinnett, and Henry counties. Within the metro, base monthly varies by roughly $1,800/month from the lowest sub-area to the highest. Here's where the median shifts:

Buckhead, Sandy Springs, and Dunwoody

Premium properties, larger units, amenity-driven communities. Base monthly typically $5,800–$6,500. Several luxury-tier communities clear $6,500 with care fees layered in.

Midtown, Atlantic Station, and Brookhaven

Mid-premium. Base monthly typically $5,200–$5,800. Newer hospital-system-affiliated communities (Emory Healthcare, Piedmont) drive this band.

Decatur, North Druid Hills, and East Atlanta

Mid-tier. Base monthly typically $4,900–$5,400. Mix of established 1990s-2000s buildings and recent renovations.

Marietta, Kennesaw, and Roswell (north Cobb)

Slightly below metro median. Base monthly typically $4,600–$5,000. Newer master-planned communities and mid-tier inventory; often the best value for moderate care needs.

Lawrenceville, Snellville, and Suwanee (Gwinnett)

Value-tier and mid-tier mix. Base monthly typically $4,400–$4,800. Older inventory in the inner suburbs, newer mid-tier in the outer Gwinnett.

Stockbridge, McDonough, and south metro suburbs

Lowest sub-area median. Base monthly typically $4,000–$4,500. Smaller communities, fewer luxury amenities, longer drive from the Perimeter and downtown.

What pushes your Atlanta assisted living bill above the median

The $5,000 base is just the rent + meals + basic services line. The real bill is the base plus add-ons. The add-ons most Atlanta families don't see until move-in:

  • Care-level fee: $400–$1,300/month, assessed at move-in based on a points system. Most Atlanta communities reassess every 90 days.
  • Medication management: $200–$400/month if staff administers daily meds. Rarely included in base.
  • Incontinence supplies and care: $100–$300/month. Some communities charge per-incident; others charge a flat add-on.
  • Two-person transfer: $300–$600/month if your family member can't transfer independently.
  • Community fee (one-time): $1,500–$5,000 at move-in. Rarely refundable.
  • Annual rent increase: 3–7% per year on the base. Care-level fees can also rise mid-year if needs change.

Realistic median + likely add-ons for a moderate-care Atlanta resident: $5,600–$6,200/month all-in. Heavy-care residents in Buckhead or Sandy Springs often clear $6,500.

Georgia CCSP and SOURCE Medicaid: who qualifies and how it works

If private-pay isn't sustainable, Georgia's Medicaid long-term care programs are CCSP (Community Care Services Program) and SOURCE (Service Options Using Resources in a Community Environment). CCSP and SOURCE are the two waiver programs that cover assisted living in Georgia for elderly and disabled adults. We're not a Medicaid-planning service. But not mentioning these waivers would be dishonest.

How CCSP and SOURCE work: CCSP serves frail elderly and physically disabled adults; SOURCE serves people 65+ who would otherwise need a nursing home. Both programs contract with participating ALFs and pay the facility directly through the assigned care management organization.

Two things many Georgia families don't know early enough:

  • CCSP and SOURCE have meaningful waitlists — Georgia's Medicaid LTC programs are funded at a level that has not historically kept pace with demand, so even financially-eligible applicants can wait 3–9 months for a slot.
  • Not every Atlanta-area assisted living facility participates. Among those that do, plan-funded beds are often concentrated outside Buckhead and Sandy Springs.

Financial eligibility (2025):

  • Countable assets: at or below $2,000 for a single applicant
  • Monthly income: at or below approximately $2,901 (300% of SSI federal benefit rate); Georgia uses Qualified Income Trusts (Miller Trusts) for over-income applicants
  • The home is generally exempt as long as the applicant or spouse intends to return; Georgia operates Medicaid Estate Recovery

Medical eligibility: the applicant must require a "nursing facility level of care" as assessed by Georgia's Department of Community Health.

If AL is going to be a 2+ year commitment for your family, get the Georgia DCH eligibility screening initiated in the first 90 days of the search, regardless of current financial position. The waitlist matters as much as the financial determination — apply early. Talk to an elder law attorney about the look-back rules before you spend down.

Six questions to ask when you tour an Atlanta assisted living community

  1. What's the last 3 years of base rent increases? If they hesitate, that's a signal worth weighing.
  2. How is the care-level fee assessed and how often is it reassessed? Ask for the points sheet. Ask what triggers a mid-year increase.
  3. What's included in base versus what's an add-on? Walk through the worksheet above line-by-line.
  4. What's your staff-to-resident ratio at night? Day ratios matter, but night ratios are where most quiet incidents happen.
  5. Do you participate in CCSP or SOURCE, and what's the current waitlist for plan-funded beds? Georgia's Medicaid LTC waitlists are real — even after financial eligibility, you may wait 3–9 months for a slot. Confirm specifics.
  6. What's your 30-day notice and refund policy if we move out or pass away? Some communities pro-rate; some don't.

If a community won't itemize the bill, won't show you the rate increase history, or won't quote a current CCSP/SOURCE waitlist, that's a signal worth weighing.
