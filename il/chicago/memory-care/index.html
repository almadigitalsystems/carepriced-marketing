Chicago sub-area pricing — what changes by neighborhood

Within the Chicago metro, base monthly memory care varies by roughly $2,700/month from the lowest sub-area to the highest. Here's where the median shifts:

North Shore (Evanston, Wilmette)

Premium properties, smaller resident-to-staff ratios, lakefront proximity. Base monthly typically $7,600–$8,200. Several luxury-tier secured units clear $10,000 with advanced behavioral care.

Lincoln Park and Gold Coast

Urban core, high-amenity. Base monthly typically $7,000–$7,500. Smaller footprints, higher per-square-foot cost, closest to downtown family neighborhoods.

Oak Park and Western Suburbs

Mid-tier. Base monthly typically $5,800–$6,300. Mix of established communities with renovated secured wings and recently opened standalone memory care.

South Suburbs (Orland Park, Tinley Park)

Below metro median. Base monthly typically $5,000–$5,500. Larger campuses, more value-tier secured units, often the best value when behavioral needs are moderate.

Northwest Suburbs (Arlington Heights, Schaumburg)

Slightly below metro median. Base monthly typically $5,400–$5,900. Suburban campuses, good highway access. If proximity to the lakefront isn't a priority and you're managing private-pay carefully, this is where the dollars stretch.

What pushes your Chicago memory care bill above the median

Memory care add-ons differ from standard AL because the secured baseline already includes a lot of what AL bills as "extra." But the bill still moves:

  • Behavioral care upgrade: $400–$1,000/month, assessed at move-in based on specific behaviors (wandering at night, sundowning, resistance to care, exit-seeking). Most Chicago communities reassess every 90 days.
  • Medication management: $200–$400/month if billed separately. Often bundled into MC base — confirm in writing.
  • Incontinence supplies and care: $100–$300/month. More commonly bundled into the MC base than in standard AL — but confirm whether supplies themselves are billed at retail.
  • Hospice overlay (when needed): Typically $0 additional from the facility (hospice is Medicare-billed) but the room continues to bill at the MC rate. Coordination details matter.
  • Community fee (one-time): $1,500–$5,000 at move-in. Rarely refundable. MC community fees often run higher than AL because of the assessment intake.
  • Annual rent increase: 3–7% per year on the base. Behavioral-care fees can also rise mid-year if needs change.

Realistic median + likely behavioral add-ons for a moderate-care Chicago MC resident: $7,200–$8,000/month all-in. Heavy-care or premium-North Shore residents clear $10,000.

Illinois Supportive Living Program (SLP): who qualifies and how it works

If private-pay isn't sustainable, Illinois's Medicaid assisted living program is called the Supportive Living Program (SLP). SLP covers memory care in Illinois, and not mentioning it would be dishonest — for many Chicago families, it's the difference between staying in a community and leaving it.

Why SLP matters more in memory care than in AL:

  • Memory care stays are typically longer and monthlies are higher, so personal assets are drawn down faster than in standard AL.
  • SLP planning done early — ideally before assets are exhausted — opens more facility options. Done late, choices narrow.
  • Not every Chicago MC community participates in SLP, and the ones that do can have waitlists, especially in North Shore and Lincoln Park areas.

Financial eligibility (2025):

  • Countable assets: at or below $2,000 for a single applicant
  • Monthly income: at or below roughly $2,829
  • The home is generally exempt as long as the applicant or spouse intends to return

Medical eligibility: the applicant must require a "nursing home level of care" as assessed by a Determination of Need (DON) screening.

What SLP pays: SLP contracts with specific memory care facilities and pays them directly. The resident keeps a small personal needs allowance; the rest of their income goes toward care.

If memory care is a likely 2–4 year commitment for your family, get an SLP pre-screening done in the first 90 days of the diagnosis, regardless of current financial position. Six questions to ask when you tour a Chicago memory care community

  1. What's your daytime caregiver-to-resident ratio in the memory care unit? This is the cost driver — and the answer tells you what you're actually buying. Anything over 1:8 deserves follow-up questions.
  2. What's your night ratio in the memory care wing? Day ratios matter, but night ratios are where most quiet incidents happen — sundowning, wandering, falls. Worth asking explicitly.
  3. How is the behavioral-care fee assessed and how often is it reassessed? Ask for the points sheet. Ask what triggers a mid-year increase.
  4. What's included in MC base versus what's an add-on? Walk through the worksheet above line-by-line.
  5. Do you accept SLP Medicaid, and if a current private-pay resident transitions to SLP, can they stay in the same secured unit? Some communities have only a small SLP contract and may move residents to a different building when they convert.
  6. What's your 30-day notice and refund policy if we move out or pass away? Some communities pro-rate; some don't. MC residents often have shorter average stays than AL, which makes this question matter more.

If a community won't itemize the bill, won't show you the rate increase history, or won't disclose the night caregiver ratio, that's a signal worth weighing.
