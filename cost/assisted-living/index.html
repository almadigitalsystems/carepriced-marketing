What three sources say about the 2026 national median

Most pages cite one number. We compare three independent surveys. They converge within 6% — which is unusually tight for senior-care pricing data and gives us confidence in the $5,350 figure.

Source National AL median (monthly) Survey year Methodology
A Place for Mom — Senior Living Cost Report1 $5,350 2026 (primary source) Proprietary pricing from partner communities across all 50 states; 14,000+ facilities surveyed
Genworth / CareScout — Cost of Care Survey2 $5,100 2024 (most recent; survey cadence changed) Phone survey of providers across 440+ markets; used as baseline for v0 Carepriced estimates
Caring.com — Senior Living Cost Guide3 $5,200 2026 Aggregated from partner directory listings; self-reported rates from communities
Why the numbers differ. Each source uses a different survey frame. APFM surveys partner communities (large national and regional chains are overrepresented). Genworth used a phone survey that reached more independent and rural operators. Caring.com aggregates self-reported rates, which can lag actual billed amounts. The 6% spread is within normal margin for a market where prices vary 30–40% within a single metro.

For families trying to budget, the most useful number is the local market median, not the national figure. A family in rural Mississippi faces a fundamentally different market than a family in coastal Connecticut. Use the national median as orientation, then use the state table below to narrow to your state, and your local cost calculator to narrow to a specific facility.

Cost drivers

What makes your specific bill higher or lower

The median is a starting point, not your number. These six factors move the actual bill by hundreds to thousands of dollars per month.

📍
Location within the state
A metro-area facility in Charlotte, NC costs ~$5,200/month. A rural western NC facility costs ~$3,400/month. Same state, $1,800 difference. Metro tier is the single largest cost driver after labor.
Impact: Very high (±30–40%)
🛏️
Room type
Private studio vs. one-bedroom vs. shared room. Shared rooms can cut the base rate by 20–30%, but are increasingly rare in newer communities. Private one-bedrooms run 15–25% above a studio at the same community.
Impact: High (±20–25%)
🩺
Level of care add-ons
Base rent covers housing and activities. Medication management, bathing assistance, dressing, and incontinence care are billed separately as "care packages." A resident needing full ADL support adds $1,000–$2,500/month above base rent.
Impact: Very high (±$1,000–$2,500)
🏗️
Facility age and amenities
A purpose-built community opened post-2015 with a fitness center, spa services, and multiple dining venues will charge 25–40% more than a converted 1980s facility with fewer amenities. New construction costs are passed through in rates.
Impact: Moderate (±20–35%)
🏢
Chain vs. independent operator
Large national chains (Brookdale, Sunrise, Atria) often charge a brand premium. Regional chains and independent operators sometimes offer lower rates — and more room to negotiate — especially if they have available units.
Impact: Moderate (±10–20%)
📋
Community fees and deposits
Most facilities charge a one-time community fee at move-in ($1,500–$5,000) plus first and last month's rent. Some charge a separate assessment fee ($500–$1,500) for the initial care-needs evaluation. These don't appear in the monthly quote.
Impact: One-time ($2,000–$7,000)

The "base rate trap" — what the quoted number doesn't include

The most common family surprise in assisted living: the tour quote covers only the base accommodation. Care services are billed monthly on top. A community might quote $3,800/month for a studio, but a resident needing help with bathing, dressing, and medication management will actually pay $5,200–$5,800/month once the care tiers are added.

When touring, always ask: "What is the total all-in monthly cost for someone with [your parent's specific needs]?" Request a sample Level of Care assessment and get the care tier cost schedule in writing before signing anything.

Financial planning

How families pay for assisted living

Most families cover assisted living through a combination of sources. Fewer than 10% pay entirely out-of-pocket from savings alone.

Veteran benefit
VA Aid & Attendance
Up to $2,727/month for veterans needing daily assistance — or up to $1,753/month for a surviving spouse. Eligibility, current amounts, and application walkthrough.
Public coverage
Medicaid HCBS waivers
43 states have Medicaid waivers that cover some assisted living costs for qualifying low-asset individuals. Waitlists can be long; applying early matters. Find your state's waiver program.
Private coverage
Long-term care insurance
If your parent holds an LTC policy, now is the time to review what it covers. Most policies pay $150–$300/day for qualified care — often enough to offset 30–60% of assisted living costs.

What about Medicare?

Medicare does not cover assisted living. It covers short-term skilled nursing facility stays (up to 100 days after a qualifying hospital stay), some home health services, and hospice — but not the custodial care (help with bathing, dressing, meals) that defines assisted living. If someone tells you Medicare will pay for assisted living, that's incorrect.

Medicaid is different. Medicaid can cover some assisted living costs through Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS) waivers in participating states — but these are means-tested programs with income and asset limits, and many states have waiting lists of 1–3 years or longer.

Frequently asked questions

Assisted living cost — common questions

The questions families most often ask when they start looking at assisted living for the first time.

What is the average cost of assisted living in 2026?

The national median is $5,350 per month in 2026, according to A Place for Mom's 2026 Senior Living Cost Report — the largest annual dataset on senior care pricing. The full range runs from about $3,300/month in the lowest-cost states (Mississippi, Alabama, Oklahoma) to over $8,400/month in Alaska and above $7,500 in Connecticut and Massachusetts.

For your specific situation, the state-level median matters more than the national figure. Use the state table above or the facility cost calculator to narrow to your market.

Does Medicare pay for assisted living?

No. Medicare does not cover assisted living. This is one of the most common misunderstandings families have early in the planning process. Medicare covers acute hospital care, short-term skilled nursing (up to 100 days post-hospitalization), and some home health services — but not the custodial care (bathing, dressing, meals, medication management) that makes up most of an assisted living bill.

Medicaid is a different program. Medicaid can cover some assisted living costs through HCBS waivers, but coverage varies by state and requires meeting income and asset thresholds. Many states have significant waitlists. Learn more about Medicaid waivers by state →

What's the difference between assisted living and memory care — and how much more does memory care cost?

Assisted living provides housing, meals, activities, and help with activities of daily living (ADLs) for seniors who need some support but not 24/7 skilled nursing. Memory care is a specialized version designed for people with Alzheimer's or other forms of dementia — it features secured (locked) environments, higher staff-to-resident ratios, and structured programming specifically for cognitive impairment.

Memory care typically costs $1,000–$1,800 more per month than standard assisted living at the same community or in the same market. The national median memory care cost is approximately $6,900/month in 2026, versus $5,350 for assisted living.

What does assisted living actually include in the monthly rate?

Base assisted living rent typically includes:

Included in most base rates: Housing (studio or one-bedroom unit), three daily meals plus snacks, housekeeping and laundry, scheduled transportation to appointments, activities and social programming, 24-hour on-site staff.

Usually billed as add-ons (not included in the base quote): Medication management, assistance with bathing, dressing, grooming, or toileting, incontinence supplies, physical therapy, personal phone or cable service, guest meals, beauty/barber services.

The add-ons are where family costs often exceed the original quote. A resident who needs daily help with bathing, dressing, and medication management may add $1,200–$2,500/month to the base rate. Always ask for the all-in cost estimate based on your parent's specific care needs before signing.

Which state has the cheapest assisted living in 2026?

Mississippi has the lowest median assisted living cost at approximately $3,300/month, followed by Alabama ($3,450/month) and Oklahoma ($3,550/month). These states have lower overall cost of living, lower labor costs, and generally older building stock in the assisted living market.

Worth noting: lower cost doesn't automatically mean lower quality, but lower-cost states also tend to have lower staffing minimums and older facilities. If you're comparing a low-cost state option (for a family member who'd be moving closer to you), factor in quality of care, not just price.

Which state has the most expensive assisted living?

Alaska is the most expensive at approximately $8,400/month median in 2026, primarily due to the extreme cost of labor, goods, and services in remote Alaskan markets. In the contiguous 48 states, Connecticut ($7,700/month) and Massachusetts ($7,150/month) are the most expensive, driven by high wages, dense regulatory environments, and strong demand in affluent markets near major Northeast cities.

Can you negotiate assisted living costs?

Yes, in many cases. Communities with available units (a meaningful vacancy rate) often have more room to negotiate. What families can sometimes negotiate:

• A waived or reduced community fee (often $2,000–$5,000 at move-in)
• Locking in the current rate before an announced increase
• Reduced rates for a second resident in the same unit (couples discount)
• Trial period terms

The published rate is rarely the only option, especially if the community has had recent vacancies. Ask directly: "What is your current occupancy rate, and is there any flexibility on the community fee?" The worst they can say is no. If a community is at or near full occupancy, negotiating room is limited.

How do families typically pay for assisted living?

Most families use a combination of sources rather than a single payment method. The most common funding mix:

Private resources: Retirement savings, Social Security income, pension income, proceeds from selling the family home (often the largest single source).

Veterans benefits: VA Aid & Attendance pays up to $2,727/month for qualifying veterans and $1,753/month for surviving spouses. This is significantly underutilized — many families don't know it's available. Check eligibility →

Long-term care insurance: If a parent bought an LTC policy before age 70, it may now pay $150–$300/day toward qualified care — potentially offsetting 30–60% of the monthly cost. Review any existing policies immediately.

Medicaid: For families who have spent down assets to qualifying levels, Medicaid HCBS waivers cover assisted living in participating states. Waitlists are common; apply as early as possible.
